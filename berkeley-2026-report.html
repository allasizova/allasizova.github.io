Report

Two months at the University of California,
Berkeley

February 9 – April 11, 2026 Alla Sizova

In 2025, I received a scholarship within the LMU–UC Berkeley Research in the Humanities Program for a research stay at the University of California, Berkeley. The project, entitled Towards a History of Tibetan Buddhism in the Tangut State: Tibetan-Language Liturgical and Ritual Collections, was carried out under the guidance of Prof. Jacob P. Dalton, Khyentse Professor in Tibetan Buddhism at UC Berkeley. The stay was undertaken on the recommendation of Prof. Vincent Tournier, Director of the Institute of Indology and Tibetology at LMU, and my academic supervisor, Marta Sernesi, Guest Professor of Tibetology and Buddhist Studies.

The support from both LMU and UCB in organizing the visit was equally smooth and efficient — and so, in early February, I found myself beneath the flowering trees of California, on the university campus.

The visit was directly connected to my dissertation, Tibetan Buddhism in the Tangut State (Study Based on the Collection of Tibetan Manuscripts from Khara-Khoto), which I am preparing as a third-year PhD student in the LMU Doctoral Program in Buddhist Studies. My work examines the diffusion of Tibetan Buddhism in the Tangut state (1038–1227) on the basis of fragmentary Tibetan-language manuscripts recovered from the ruins of Khara-Khoto in the early twentieth century. Prof. Dalton's expertise in tantric ritual texts and Dunhuang manuscripts intersects closely with the core themes of my project, and his guidance significantly advanced my analysis of liturgical and ritual collections, as well as the comparative study of Khara-Khoto and Dunhuang manuscript exemplars. I also had the opportunity to consult with Prof. Alexander von Rospatt (Buddhist and South Asian Studies) and Prof. Brian Baumann (East Asian Studies).

The Bay Area offered more than academic life: a visit to the Asian Art Museum in San Francisco, with its fine collection of Tibetan art, made for a welcome counterpoint to the library and seminar room.

I participated actively in the academic life of the university, attending reading groups in Tibetan, Sanskrit, and Classical Mongolian. On April 2, 2026, I gave a talk entitled The Role of Tibetan Texts and Language in Tangut Culture to the Buddhist Studies Research Group; the discussion that followed will be reflected in the dissertation. At the East Asian Library, I was able to access facsimile editions of the Tangut manuscripts — sources of exceptional importance to my research that are difficult to obtain elsewhere.

The ties between the Institute of Indology and Tibetology at LMU and the Buddhist Studies Research Group at Berkeley have a long history, and it was a privilege to be part of that ongoing connection. Work on the conceptual part of my dissertation undoubtedly benefited from the sustained exchanges with colleagues at Berkeley.

Alla Sizova · 2026 Munich, Germany
